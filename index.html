Vitamin C — safety review

A plain-language drug-safety check on ascorbic acid (vitamin C), across pills, IV drips and skin creams. Every number below is real and traceable — built from live medical-research and regulator data, not estimates.

Run 2026-04-24 Updated 2026-06-11 1,211 papers read down to 142 402,567 FDA reports 15 US recalls
The bottom line

At normal everyday amounts, vitamin C is safe. The real risks show up at very high doses — IV drips and megadose pills — and in a few specific groups (people with the inherited G6PD condition, kidney problems, or those taking huge amounts long-term). The single biggest signal is kidney damage from too much; the most surprising is that high-dose IV vitamin C can fool glucose meters into false readings.

6 Confirmed risks

In plain words

These are the real, established risks — already on official labels and now backed by the papers we found. They mostly matter at high doses or in specific groups.

confirmed
Kidney damage from too much
Big IV doses or megadose pills form crystals that injure the kidneys. The strongest signal — 13 real cases, FDA score 2.33, with a COVID-era spike.
confirmed
Fools glucose meters
High-dose IV vitamin C makes finger-prick meters read falsely high. Two ICU cases led to wrong diabetes treatment — ranked #1 and #4 in our top papers.
confirmed
Blood-cell breakdown (G6PD)
People with the inherited G6PD condition can have red blood cells break apart on high doses. Highest FDA score of all — 3.48.
confirmed
Skin allergy from creams
Some vitamin C creams cause allergic rashes — confirmed by patch testing (seven cases for the ingredient 3-O-ethyl ascorbic acid).
confirmed
Stomach upset at high doses
Doses above ~2 g/day commonly cause diarrhoea, nausea and cramps. Well known and reversible.
confirmed
Contaminated IV products
15 US recalls of IV vitamin C — mostly unsafe compounding-pharmacy batches; one had glass particles.
